# Transportation Basics (Japan)

**Last reviewed:** July 2026

Enough to help the child plan a realistic route. Adults own booking, rail-pass value, and
any accessibility verification.

## Trains are the backbone

- **Shinkansen** ("bullet trains") connect the big cities quickly and comfortably.
- Local trains and subways cover the cities. Use a map's **"Directions" tool to get travel
  *times*** rather than eyeballing distance -- time is what matters for a route.
- **IC cards** are tap-to-pay cards for trains and buses. Which visitor options exist and
  work changes often (physical and mobile versions, adding a card to a phone wallet), and
  mobile cards can be hard to add on some phones. **Verify which options currently work for a
  visitor** rather than memorizing today's list.
- Whether a **rail pass** is worth it changes too. Adults verify current value and
  eligibility; the child only needs to know a pass may exist.

## The walking and stairs reality (a pacing factor)

Japan trips often mean a lot of walking -- commonly 15,000 to 20,000 steps a day -- and
station transfers can involve long walks and many stairs. **Not every station has an
elevator.** This matters for pacing, especially for anyone who tires more easily and in
summer heat. Step-free routes and station elevators exist but vary and must be checked --
that verification is a grown-up's job; the child just flags likely trouble spots.

## Handy planning ideas

- **Takkyubin (luggage forwarding)** -- your bags can be sent ahead to your next hotel, so
  you do not carry them on stairs. Great for a family with a lot of luggage.
- **Coin lockers** -- at many stations, for stashing bags for the day.
- On the main Shinkansen lines, **oversized baggage may need a reserved spot** -- verify the
  current size rule; a big family's luggage can hit it, which makes forwarding even handier.

## Getting there and the arrival day

Flights are adult-owned. Plan the **arrival day gently** -- it comes after a long flight and
a big time change (roughly 14 to 15 hours for families in the US), so the first two or three
days can feel jet-lagged. Getting from the airport into the city is part of that first day;
a grown-up sorts the airport transit.
